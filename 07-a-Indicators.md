## Indicators

### Indicator scores

Indicators can have scores for reliability, validity, and sensitivity. These scores influence the overall score of the impact claims based on them.

1. Validity
a. Does it measure what you think it measures?
Does the indicator actually reflect the underlying concept?
Example: Resting heart rate is a reasonably valid indicator of cardiovascular fitness — but not of mental health.
If an indicator is only loosely related, it becomes misleading.

b. Is it uniquely tied to the thing? (Specificity)
Does it change only when the underlying factor changes—or for many unrelated reasons?
Example: Fatigue could indicate illness, stress, poor sleep, or overtraining → low specificity.
Low specificity leads to ambiguity.

2. Reliability
Is it consistent?
If you measure it repeatedly under similar conditions, do you get similar results?
Example: Body weight measured on a stable scale is reliable; mood ratings might fluctuate heavily day to day.
Unreliable indicators are noisy and hard to act on.

3. Sensitivity 
a. Does it change when the underlying reality changes?
Can it detect meaningful differences or changes?
Example: Blood glucose is very sensitive to metabolic changes; height is not useful for short-term health monitoring.
If it doesn’t move when reality changes, it's useless for tracking.

b. Does it update fast enough? (Timeliness)
Does it reflect changes in a useful timeframe?
Example: Blood pressure responds relatively quickly; long-term disease risk scores do not.
Delayed indicators are poor for feedback loops.
